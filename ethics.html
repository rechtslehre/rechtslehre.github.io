This course will address the questions:

  • what is rationality? or equivalently: what is practical wisdom?
  • what is morality?
    • or more narrowly: what is justice?
        that is: what is it to wrong someone, or to do right by her?
  • what things are wrong?
    • or, more narrowly, what things are just and unjust?
        that is: what are the forms of injustice, or what ways can we wrong others?

and, above all, the question that puts the central concepts together:

  • is justice rational?
    • or equivalently: is the wise person also just?
    • that is, would a genuinely wise person do genuine wrong by another?
    or, instead, is justice some kind of fraud that only really stupid people would fall for?

It should go with saying that we will manage only a very preliminary development of these themes.

Requirements

There will be one short paper, on Plato's Crito, one in-class test on justice, benevolence and consequentialism, another on the Platonic immoralists and a take-home essay on Plato's own theory in the Republic. The final exam will be cumulative but mostly focus on Kant's Groundwork.

The texts for the course are linked below. It is important to print these, and bring them to lecture and section, as philosophy is characterized by close attention to the precise wording of the text and especially the precise succession of sentences in argument. The page count of the readings is low, and so each work should be read carefully.

The grade will be divided equally among these things, tests (3), written essays (2) and your contribution to discussion in section.
